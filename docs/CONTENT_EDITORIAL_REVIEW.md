# Content Editorial Review

_Last updated: 2026-05-17._

## Sample audited

Reviewed representative seed patterns from 10 lessons, 10 exercises, 10 labs, 5 guided projects, and glossary samples in the mock/backend curriculum. The content is safely defensive and broad enough for beta, but still needs human voice, classroom examples, and learner feedback.

## Quality rubric

Score every item 1–5:

1. **Beginner clarity** — Can a 14–18 year old explain the main idea after reading it once?
2. **Direct action** — Does the learner know what to do next?
3. **Safety** — Does it avoid live-target instructions, credential theft, exploit chaining, malware, or scanning real systems?
4. **Evidence** — Does it ask for observable facts before conclusions?
5. **Brevity** — Is it short enough for a real session?
6. **Human tone** — Does it avoid fake corporate wording and generic filler?
7. **Assessment fit** — Does the question/lab actually test the stated objective?

## How to review lessons

- Keep intros under 120 words.
- Use one concrete school-safe example.
- Define jargon before using it.
- End with a clear action: answer a check, inspect toy evidence, write a short note, or create an artifact.
- Remove claims that imply job readiness from one short lesson.

## How to review labs

- Verify the dataset is fictional and labeled as fictional.
- Verify tasks require defensive reasoning, not attack execution.
- Include expected evidence and a safe next step.
- Add hints for beginners before they submit.
- Ensure scoring rewards clarity, authorization, and evidence.

## How to spot generated-looking content

- Vague phrases such as “robust comprehensive solution” without a concrete action.
- Long paragraphs that repeat the same idea.
- Examples that do not match student context.
- Rubrics that mention evidence but do not name the evidence.
- Overconfident career claims.
- Security topics presented as tricks instead of responsible defensive practice.

## Preparing content for real students

1. Read aloud and cut anything that sounds like marketing.
2. Ask one student to paraphrase the task.
3. Add one concrete example and one non-example.
4. Confirm the safest answer is the easiest answer.
5. Add a teacher note only when it helps facilitation.
6. Mark content `reviewed` only after human review and at least one learner observation.
